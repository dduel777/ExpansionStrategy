Recommended approach

Therapy or psych group sublease is your best path. Reach out to solo therapists or small group practices in each target city. They often have underutilized rooms and welcome monthly income. You get a clinical address, occasional in-person capacity, and a natural referral relationship.

Structure it as a formal sublease (not just a handshake): defined hours, mail handling rights, signage permission, HIPAA BAA if the landlord might access PHI, and right to list the address publicly.

Payer contracting implications

How multi-location works in CAQH and NPPES

Each physical address needs to be registered as a practice location. Here's what that looks like across each system:

SystemWhat you updateHow oftenNotes
NPPES Add each address as a secondary practice location under your group NPI (Type II) and each provider's individual NPI (Type I) Before contracting each location NPPES updates propagate to payer directories. Inconsistency is the #1 audit red flag.
CAQH ProView Add each practice location in the "Practice Locations" section. Upload sublease or lease evidence if requested. At attestation (every 120 days) Payers pull from CAQH. A location not in CAQH will delay or block credentialing.
Payer directories Submit location addition requests to each payer's provider relations team after credentialing Per location addition Most payers add 30–90 days processing time per location. Budget for this in rollout timelines.
Group contract (TIN) Your group TIN contract typically allows additional locations — confirm with each payer. Some require a contract amendment; others just need a location addition form. Per location Blue Shield, Anthem, and Aetna each have different location amendment processes. Ask payer relations proactively.

What a credible setup looks like to a payer

  • Address matches across NPPES, CAQH, and payer directory
  • Physical location verifiable by site visit (rare but happens)
  • Local phone number (or Zoom Phone extension routed to that "market")
  • Address consistent on your website and Google Business Profile
  • Sublease or lease documentation available if audited

What looks suspicious to a payer

Red flags that trigger payer audits or directory removals: address is a UPS Store or Regus mailbox; multiple provider NPIs listing the same non-clinical address; address added within 30 days of a billing spike; no local presence evidence (no GBP, no website mention).

Telehealth service area vs. practice location

This distinction matters: a practice location is where you are listed as based and credentialed. A telehealth service area is where patients are located. You do not need a physical address in every county you serve — only in the markets where you want local directory presence and SEO lift. Credential selectively.

Google Business Profile and local SEO

What qualifies for a GBP listing in healthcare

Google's guidelines for healthcare providers require a staffed physical location where patients can be seen. Specifically:

  • The address must be a real office, not a P.O. box, UPS Store, or shared mailbox
  • The location must be staffed during stated hours, or have a clear way to reach the practice
  • Telehealth-only practices can list a physical address if they have a legitimate office at that address — even if visits are rare
  • You must be able to receive patients at that address if requested
Practical interpretation: a therapy sublease where you could see a patient for an in-person intake (even once a month) qualifies. A mailbox address does not. The test Google applies is: could a patient walk in and find your practice here?

Location pages: avoiding thin/duplicate content

Each city landing page must be substantively different from the others. The bare minimum per page:

  • City-specific intro paragraph (local landmarks, community context, prevalence data if available)
  • Local provider bios if applicable (even a sentence about their connection to that area)
  • Insurance coverage specific to that market (e.g., Medi-Cal managed care plans for certain counties)
  • Local FAQ (e.g., "Do you see patients near [landmark/hospital]?")
  • Embedded Google Map of the local address
  • Unique meta title and description — not templated with city name swapped

Pages that only swap the city name are flagged as doorway pages by Google and may be penalized. Budget for 300–500 words of unique content per city page, ideally written by someone with local knowledge or lightly customized from a template by a VA.

Compliance checklist

Items to review with healthcare counsel before scaling. This is not legal advice — it's a scoping list.

Lease / sublease

  • Right to use address in all marketing, NPPES, CAQH, and payer directories explicitly stated in writing
  • Right to receive mail at address (including payer correspondence, credentialing mail)
  • Right to place signage (even small — a nameplate or suite label is usually sufficient for payers)
  • Defined minimum access hours (even 4 hrs/week) for occasional in-person use
  • HIPAA Business Associate Agreement if host has any possibility of accessing PHI (shared staff, shared check-in areas, visible records)
  • Termination clause: 30-day notice minimum to protect your credentialing continuity
  • Insurance: confirm your professional liability and general liability cover the sublease location

HIPAA

  • BAA with any co-tenant or building staff who might encounter PHI
  • Mail handling SOP: all payer correspondence and patient mail forwarded to PsychBright within 24–48 hours
  • Physical security: if any clinical work happens at the location, ensure the room can be private

California-specific

  • Fictitious Business Name (FBN) / DBA: if doing business as "PsychBright Health" at a new county address, may require a county-level FBN filing. Check with your attorney — low cost (<$100/county) but often overlooked.
  • Medical Corporation rules: California prohibits lay ownership of medical corporations. Confirm your existing corporate structure covers operation in new locations without additional filings.
  • DMHC / Knox-Keene: if you ever take on capitated or managed care risk contracts, additional licensure may apply. Not relevant for fee-for-service telehealth.
  • Medi-Cal provider enrollment: if you plan to accept Medi-Cal in new locations, each location may require separate enrollment. Coordinate with your biller.

Payer audit risk

  • Maintain lease/sublease docs on file — payers can request proof of practice location
  • Ensure all providers billing from a new address are credentialed at that address before billing
  • Do not list an address on a payer directory until the space is actually secured
Bottom line for counsel: you need a healthcare-savvy real estate attorney to review sublease language, and your healthcare compliance attorney to sign off on the payer credentialing approach and FBN question. One-time review per template, not per location.

Lean operating model — day-to-day across sites

Mail handling

Every sublease location will receive payer mail, occasional patient correspondence, and credentialing notices. Your options:

  • Best option: Include in sublease that host office scans and emails all incoming mail to a designated PsychBright Gmail alias (e.g., losangeles@psychbrighthealth.com) within 24 hours. Pay a small handling fee ($25–50/mo) if needed.
  • Backup: VA drives to the location weekly to pick up mail. Only viable within your existing VA coverage area (Beverly Hills / LA metro).
  • For distant markets: Use a local 1099 contractor (often a therapist admin) for mail pickup at $50–75/mo.

Phone routing

You're already on Zoom Phone. Assign a local DID (Direct Inward Dial) number for each new market. Route it to your existing call queues. Cost is minimal (~$5/mo per DID). The local number displays on GBP and the website, improving local SEO and patient trust. Do not get a separate phone system per location.

VA workflows

Your Philippines VA team doesn't change — they work centrally. What changes per new location:

  • New Gmail alias monitored in shared inbox
  • New GBP listing claimed and maintained (respond to reviews, update hours)
  • Credentialing tracker in Notion updated with new location statuses
  • Payer directory check: quarterly audit that each location appears correctly in Anthem, Blue Shield, Aetna, etc.

What happens if a patient shows up in person

This will happen. Prepare for it. Your sublease host needs to know the protocol: patients are seen by appointment only, telehealth practice, all visits virtual. Host staff gives them a card with your scheduling line (ext. 810) and explains the model. You are not abandoning patients — you're redirecting them to the correct intake channel.

Optionally, you can offer occasional in-person intakes at the sublease location for patients who specifically request it. Even 2–4 per month per location validates the physical presence and strengthens payer audit defensibility.

Lease/sublease — required provisions checklist

This is your minimum viable lease checklist. Have counsel review any agreement against these before signing.

  • Address use rights — explicit written permission to use the address in payer credentialing filings, NPPES, CAQH, Google Business Profile, and all public marketing materials
  • Mail receipt rights — right to receive mail at the address; defined forwarding protocol (timeline, method)
  • Signage rights — right to place a nameplate, suite label, or small sign at or near the entrance. Even a 4"x6" nameplate qualifies for most payer audits.
  • Minimum access hours — defined weekly or monthly block for occasional in-person use. Even 4 hours/week is sufficient.
  • HIPAA BAA — if the host has any staff who could encounter PHI (shared check-in area, visible records, shared printers), execute a HIPAA BAA with the entity or individual.
  • Termination notice — minimum 30-day written notice required to terminate. 60 days preferred. Protects credentialing continuity — payer directory removals take time.
  • No exclusivity restriction — confirm the sublease doesn't restrict you from operating in the same specialty or geographic area. Some therapy groups include non-compete-style clauses.
  • No assignment of liability — you are not taking on liability for the host's premises beyond your direct use. Confirm your general liability policy covers the sublease space.
  • Sublease acknowledgment — if the host is themselves a tenant (not the building owner), confirm their prime lease allows subleasing and that you have written acknowledgment from them.
  • Address consistency clause — note in the agreement the exact address format you will use publicly (Suite #, floor, etc.) to prevent NAP inconsistencies later.
One-time counsel review of your sublease template (~$500–800) is worth it. Once the template is vetted, you can execute each new location with minimal additional legal cost — just adapt the template and have a quick review if terms differ materially.

3. Payer Contracting — ChatGPT Detail

Payers care whether a location is real, operational, associated with your TIN/NPI/group, available to patients as represented, correct in directories, and consistent across CAQH, NPPES, payer portals, claims, and your website.

CAQH practice locations

Only add a satellite site to CAQH if PsychBright can truthfully answer: "A patient can schedule an appointment associated with this location, and the practice has a real operational relationship to this site." CAQH practice location reconciliation was developed specifically because location errors are a major source of directory inaccuracies.

NPPES

NPPES distinguishes between a business mailing address and a business practice location address, and requires at least one business practice location. Do not add every marketing city to NPPES — add locations that are genuinely part of the operating footprint.

Medicare / Medicare Advantage

Having a real enrolled group/practice location helps avoid the appearance that the entire operation is floating without a physical practice base. But adding many questionable locations creates the opposite risk.

5. Compliance Checklist — ChatGPT Detail

Corporate / naming issues (California-specific)

California's Medical Board notes that a fictitious name permit allows a physician to practice under a name other than their own. Practices providing services only through telehealth using a fictitious name must post the FNP on their telehealth website or portal so patients can view it. Counsel should review: FNP/DBA structure, whether each satellite affects FNP display, local business licenses, city business tax registration, and PC address records.

Telehealth consent and patient disclosures

The Medical Board of California requires verbal and written informed consent before delivering healthcare via telemedicine, with signed written consent becoming part of the medical record. Review: telehealth consent language, website disclosures, emergency protocol by patient location, patient location verification at each visit, in-person care alternatives, and cross-state licensing if expanding beyond California.

Patient arrival protocol

Every location needs a written answer to: "What happens if a patient physically shows up?"

  1. Host staff greets patient
  2. Host verifies patient has appointment with PsychBright
  3. Host does not collect clinical details
  4. Host calls/texts PsychBright central line
  5. VA confirms appointment
  6. If no in-person provider is available, patient is offered telehealth support or rescheduling
  7. Emergency concerns go to 911/crisis protocol
