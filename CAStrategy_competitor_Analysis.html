Phase 1 Fill Current Capacity Before Adding Overhead Now → Month 6
Key insight: At 900 encounters/month across ~15 mostly part-time providers, there is almost certainly significant unused capacity in your existing provider base before you need to hire. Filling that capacity costs near zero overhead. Adding a new sublease location before you know your actual capacity ceiling means adding fixed costs prematurely.

Understand Your Real Capacity Ceiling First

  • Run a provider-level capacity audit in Valant: for each provider, calculate scheduled hours vs. available hours vs. actual encounters last 90 days. This tells you exactly how much volume the current team can absorb before you need more providers or more locations.
  • Part-time providers with open schedule slots are your cheapest growth lever. Convert 3–4 part-timers to higher utilization before adding headcount. A provider seeing 8 patients/week who could see 14 is a significant revenue gap that costs you nothing to close.
Capacity math benchmark: A full-time NP running 8 clinical hrs/day with 30-min follow-ups and 1-hr intakes, at a 70% intake / 30% follow-up mix in a ramp phase, can handle roughly 12–14 encounters/day = ~250/month. At steady state with mostly follow-ups (20–30 min), closer to 300–350/month. Your 900 encounters across 15 part-time providers suggests average utilization of ~60 encounters/month — well below any full-time ceiling. Utilization optimization is the Phase 1 growth lever.

Close the Humana MA Gap

  • Humana Medicare Advantage has significant CA market share, particularly in SoCal and among the 65+ demographic — a high-value patient segment for outpatient psychiatry. Getting in-network should be a near-term priority alongside sublease expansion.
  • Contact Humana provider relations directly. Have your group NPI, TIN, existing payer contract list, and CAQH attestation current before the call. Credentialing timeline: typically 90–120 days from application.

The Medi-Cal Decision

  • Medi-Cal is a strategic fork, not a simple gap to close. It brings high volume and access mission — but also lower reimbursement (typically 40–60% of commercial rates for psychiatry), complex billing workflows, and a different patient acuity profile that can strain clinical operations if not structured separately.
  • The right question is not "should we take Medi-Cal" but "can we operationally ring-fence Medi-Cal capacity without it degrading the commercial patient experience?" Some groups run a separate Medi-Cal clinic track. Others reserve a fixed % of slots. Do not mix it into general scheduling without a deliberate structure.
  • Recommend: defer Medi-Cal enrollment until Phase 2 infrastructure is in place and you have a clear capacity buffer to absorb the operational load.

Patient Retention and No-Show Optimization

  • Before any marketing spend to drive new intakes, audit your current retention rate: what % of patients who complete an intake return for a 90-day follow-up? Industry average for telehealth psychiatry is 55–65%. A rate below that means you are filling a leaky bucket — new patients are replacing churned patients rather than growing the panel.
  • In GoHighLevel: set up automated appointment reminders at 72 hours and 24 hours before follow-up visits. This alone typically reduces no-show rates by 20–35% without changing anything clinical.
  • In Valant: flag patients with a lapsed recall interval (no appointment in 90+ days after a prior visit). These are warm patients who likely still need care — a simple outreach campaign through GoHighLevel can reactivate them with minimal effort.
Phase 2 Build the Local Footprint — SEO and PCP Infrastructure Months 3–12
Critical reframe from the original document: Because your payer contracts already cover all of California statewide, sublease nodes are not about unlocking credentialing access. They are marketing infrastructure — specifically, the physical address required to claim a Google Business Profile listing and the local presence that gives PCPs a "home" to refer to. The ROI metric is not "payer activation" but "local search ranking and referral relationship maturation," which takes 3–6 months per market.

Target Market Selection — Where to Plant Nodes First

  • Orange County (Irvine / Newport Beach): High commercial insurance density, strong Medicare Advantage penetration, affluent demographics that match your premium positioning, manageable distance from Beverly Hills HQ for occasional in-person.
  • San Diego (North County or Central): Large psychiatry-underserved population, strong PCP referral ecosystem, high concentrations of military families and veterans — PTSD and adjustment disorder are high-volume service lines you can serve.
  • Inland Empire (Riverside / Corona / Ontario): Access shortage market, lower office costs for subleases, large commercial insurance population, less competitive for psychiatry than LA/OC.
  • Sequence: OC first (easiest to manage from BH, highest commercial density), then SD, then IE. One new location per 6–8 weeks maximum to allow GBP verification and initial PCP outreach to stabilize before adding another.

Sublease Node Setup — Revised Checklist

  • Sublease signed (therapy or psych group preferred; medical office second) → NPPES secondary location added → CAQH practice location updated → Payer directory location addition submitted (required even with statewide contracts — directory presence by city is what drives local search visibility) → Valant location added → Zoom DID assigned ($5/mo) → Gmail alias created → GBP listing claimed → Location page live on website with 300–500 words unique local content.
  • GBP is your single most important SEO deliverable per node. The listing must show the physical address, have a local phone number, be verified by postcard or video, and have defined hours that match actual sublease access. Do not rush this — a suspended GBP takes weeks to recover.
  • Stagger GBP listings at least 2–3 weeks apart. Google flags accounts that create multiple new healthcare listings rapidly.

PCP Referral Program — Systematize Before You Scale It

  • For each new sublease market, build a target list of 15–20 primary care practices within 5 miles. Your VA team can pull these from Healthgrades, Zocdoc provider search, and Google Maps in 2–3 hours per market.
  • The referral pitch is simple and must be deliverable: "We accept your patients' insurance, we respond to referrals within 48 hours, and you'll receive a consult note within 5 business days of the first appointment." This is the promise most CA psychiatry groups cannot make. If you can make it and consistently deliver it, you will own those referral relationships.
  • Track referral sources in GoHighLevel. Tag every new patient intake with a referral source. This tells you which PCPs are converting referrals and which markets have the strongest PCP pipeline — informing where to open the next node.
  • Consider a quarterly PCP outreach mailer per market: a single-page clinical update (not marketing collateral — an actual clinical communication about what conditions you treat, what your intake process looks like, and how to refer). This is what builds referring relationships with physicians, not flyers.

Location Page Strategy — Avoid Foresight's Mistake

  • Each city page needs 300–500 words of genuinely unique content. City-name swaps in a template are penalized by Google as doorway pages. Content should reference local community context, specific payer plans available in that county, nearby landmarks or hospitals PCPs would recognize, and local FAQ (e.g., "Do you accept Blue Shield Promise in Riverside County?").
  • Every page needs a consistent NAP (Name, Address, Phone) that exactly matches GBP, NPPES, and any payer directory listing. A single character difference between these sources is enough to suppress local search rankings.
Phase 3 Provider Growth — Hire Into Proven Markets Months 9–18
Sequence matters: Hire after you have demonstrated patient demand in a market — not before. Every competitor that scaled too fast hired ahead of volume and ended up with underutilized providers, low morale, and either forced churn or unsustainable overhead. Your sublease nodes generate the referral traffic and local search volume that proves market demand. Then you hire to meet it.

Part-Time to Full-Time Conversion Track

  • Your current part-time provider base is a natural growth lever. Build a clear progression: PT provider → demonstrated patient demand in their schedule → offer additional hours → convert to FT with benefits at the right volume threshold.
  • This is lower-risk than new hires: you already know their clinical quality, patients know them, and the ramp time to full panel is much shorter for an existing provider than a new hire starting from zero.

Why the Thesis Works Now

  • Solo CA psychiatrists and small groups (2–5 providers) are aging out, burning out on billing admin, or simply looking for a responsible exit. Many have existing patient panels, payer contracts, and physical addresses.
  • You already have what makes acquisition operationally feasible: statewide payer contracts your group TIN can extend, a VA admin team that can absorb their billing and scheduling, Valant as the receiving EHR, and an established clinical brand to transfer patients into.
  • An acquisition gives you instantly: a patient panel (revenue from day one), a physical address (solves sublease problem in that market), existing payer contracts (may include Humana MA or Medi-Cal you don't currently have), and a local clinical reputation.

Guardrails — Learn From Mindpath's Mistakes

  • One acquisition at a time. Minimum 6 months between transactions initially. Every acquisition creates a 90-day integration burden on your admin team — don't stack them.
  • Ideal target: solo MD or small group (2–4 providers), active payer contracts with 2–3 commercial plans plus Medicare, 100–400 established patients, physical address in a target CA market, clean compliance record.
  • Have healthcare M&A counsel review each deal for Stark, Anti-Kickback, CA Medical Corporation rules, and CAQH/payer contract assignment rights before signing anything.
  • Do not acquire more than you can fully onboard in 90 days. Mindpath's failure was acquiring faster than their admin could absorb the intake volume.

The Quality Floor

Intake length
60 minutes — no exceptions under volume pressure. This is the single most important clinical quality signal. Every competitor that compressed intakes to 45 or 30 minutes created downstream diagnostic errors and patient dissatisfaction that showed up in reviews within 12 months.
Follow-up length
30 minutes standard. 20 minutes for experienced providers at appropriate acuity (stable, well-established patients on maintenance medication). Not a blanket default for all providers or all patients. Triage criteria for 20-min slots should be defined and enforced in Valant scheduling rules.
Panel size guardrail
Capacity-based, not a fixed number. The signal to watch is recall interval drift — when stable patients are waiting longer than clinically appropriate between visits because schedules are compressed. Build this as a Valant report and review it monthly.
Provider-patient continuity
No reassignment without patient knowledge and consent, except genuine emergencies. This is a promise you can make that LifeStance (7,000 providers, high churn) and Geode (repeated layoffs) structurally cannot. Make it a standing written policy.
Billing standards
Insurance verified 48 hrs pre-appointment. Copay confirmed before visit. Zero claims older than 30 days without submission. Weekly audit. This is your most visible differentiator from LifeStance and Mindpath, whose billing failures are documented at the federal level.
Communication SLA
Portal messages: 24-hour response. Billing disputes: acknowledged 48 hrs, resolved 10 business days. Foresight's 1-hour phone wait times and Mindpath's unreachable billing department turned basic responsiveness into a marketing opportunity. You already have the VA team to deliver this.
Primary risk to monitor

The Revenue Pressure Moment

The failure mode for PsychBright is not a competitor taking your market share — it's internal. The moment you face a revenue shortfall and consider compressing intakes below 60 minutes, defaulting all follow-ups to 20 minutes regardless of acuity, or loosening billing standards to close a quarterly gap. That decision — made once, under pressure, with good justification — is how every competitor lost their quality positioning. Foresight loosened ADHD evaluation standards for revenue. LifeStance loosened billing oversight to hit margins. Geode loosened hiring standards to fill capacity. Your lean overhead model (sublease vs. owned offices, part-time providers, VA admin rather than full staff) is your protection against ever facing this decision under acute financial pressure. Protect that cost structure aggressively as you scale.
